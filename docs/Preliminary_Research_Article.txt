Lightweight Hand Pose Tracking on Edge 
Devices for Real-Time HCI using 2D and 
Stereo Image input 

Brandon MacGillivray 
School of Electronics, Electrical Engineering and Computer Science 
Queen’s University Belfast, Belfast, United Kingdom 
Email: bmacgillivray01@qub.ac.uk 

Keywords: Hand pose estimation; human–computer interaction; edge devices; stereo vision; lightweight deep 
learning 

Abstract 

Hand pose tracking can provide a natural feeling, contactless interaction for applications such as AR, VR, 
medical imaging and touchless control of everyday applications, but many current systems rely on desktop 
GPUs or become too slow when deployed on edge devices. This project investigates lightweight hand pose 
tracking for everyday HCI on GPU specific embedded devices such as an NVIDIA Jetson platform combined 
with a ZED 2 stereo camera. The objectives are to study performance and practical limitations of current hand 
pose models such as MediaPipe Hands on edge hardware. To design and implement potential promising 
lightweight pipelines for 2D and stereo RGB input informed by recent pose tracking advancements. And  
assess how pose quality and input type affect usability in interaction tasks. The methodology combines 
benchmarking of existing models with development of RGB plus stereo, lightweight focused architectures, 
using measures such as frame rate, end-to-end latency, tracking stability and range of effective operation 
under varied lighting and perspective. These metrics are complemented by observational feedback from 
testing by users performing touchless interaction tasks. The desired outcome is an effective prototype and a 
set of learned design improvements that clarify when hand pose interaction is feasible on modern edge 
devices, and where stereo sensing provides meaningful advantages over single-RGB input for effective use. 

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Introduction 

Human-computer interaction is a discipline concerned 
with the design, evaluation and implementation of 
interactive computing systems for human use and with 
the study of major phenomena surrounding them. It is a 
combination of multiple considerations, the application of 
computer science with its focus on computation, 
compared to the consideration of ergonomics, ensuring 
the interaction is comfortable and intuitive for the user. 
Its focus spans from the typical keyboard and mouse we 
know of well, to touchscreens, voice assistants, 
immersive AR/VR environments and now touchless 
visual interaction.[1] pose based HCI occupies a 
particular niche in where the main effort of the 
computation is focused on inferring a 2d-3d skeleton of a 
person's body or body part from various sensors such as 
RGB or depth cameras. Then use this as a 
representation as a basis of interaction between the user 
and the machine [6] 

Within the area of pose interaction a person's hands are 
particularly attractive as they are the primary manner of 
physical manipulation and possess a notable capacity 
for communicative gestures such as pointing, counting, 
sign language and thus abstract spatial interaction. 
Surveys of vision based hand-gesture HCI suggest that 
hand input may provide a natural, expressive and 
contact free alternative to keyboards, mice and 
controllers. This is particularly useful when a user's 
hands are already occupied or surfaces must remain 
sterile [4], [5]. Hand-pose HCI goes beyond recognising 
a simple vocabulary of discrete gestures to estimating a 
skeleton like hand model, typically represented as 21 
keypoints per hand, from RGB or depth images and 
using this real time pose tracking for selection and 
manipulation. Recent systems such as MediaPipe 
Hands demonstrate near real-time prediction of 2.5D 
hand landmarks from a single RGB camera in a 
lightweight manner explicitly targeting AR/VR and mobile 
HCI scenarios [6]. Lightweight architectures like 
Attention! are another example of an attempt to further 
reduce parameters and computation while maintaining 
competitive accuracy, making hand-pose estimation 
feasible on embedded GPUs and edge devices [7]. 

Current hand-pose models span several application 
domains. This may include medical and assistive 
settings, where vision-based gesture systems enable 
clinicians to observe radiology images or control 
operating displays without touching potentially 
contaminated surfaces, motivated by the hygiene and 
workflow constraints of a medical setting [5]. Further 
applications include assistance with 3d modelling and 
design, rehabilitation for motor and cognitive training, 
general computer input, large information display 
navigation, robot interaction and teleoperation, video 

game control, in-car controls and sign language input[2]. 
In AR/VR, camera-based hand tracking supports object 
manipulation, menu interaction and meaningful gestures, 
replacing dedicated controllers and increasing the sense 
of immersiveness of the user.[3], [8].  

Looking ahead, hand-pose HCI is expected to play a 
central role in everyday computing. Egocentric 
hand-pose and action recognition from wearable 
cameras could support assistance in activities of daily 
living, training and rehabilitation. To be more specific, 
there is potential for the use of hand pose to be used 
from an egocentric viewpoint for supervision to train 
robots how to grasp and manipulate objects, perform 
multi-step tasks, such as assembling and learn how to 
react to occlusion. Furthermore there is potential for 
automatic skill analysis for tasks requiring hand 
manipulation. This all assumes robust tracking is 
possible in cluttered first-person scenes [10]. In AR 
glasses and mixed-reality headsets, accurate 3D 
hand-pose estimation promises direct manipulation of 
virtual objects, spatial authoring and richer gestural 
languages [3], [8]. This kind of interaction already exists. 
but is yet to be considered ideal for a completely 
immersive experience.    

However, these future visions remain only somewhat 
realised. From a technology perspective, 3D hand-pose 
estimation using everyday imagery is quite difficult 
because of occlusion, motion blur, perspective 
distortions and limited "in the wild" 3D annotated training 
data [10]. The high precision models that do exist 
typically rely on deep CNN-transformer backbones and 
multi-stage refinement. And although they achieve 
notably high accuracy metrics on powerful desktop 
GPUs, an attempt to port them to more portable and 
accessible edge devices incur prohibitive latency and 
memory usage [7], [9]. Research reviews of 
gesture-based interfaces highlight further problems 
including physical fatigue, lack of standardised gesture 
sets and social acceptability concern [2]. One can 
expand this by suggesting users with irregular hand 
shape such as missing fingers could be unable to use 
models which don't take this into account during training. 
Another issue is the impact of data project laws such as 
GDPR, These hand pose trackers require capturing 
identifiable data of a person in order to create the digital 
hand representation. There exists rules regarding 
consent of use and storage of data which any provider of 
the HCI technology must consider. Together, these 
factors explain why hand-pose HCI has not yet become 
an every day tool and motivates research into robust, 
low-latency hand-pose tracking for embedded GPUs and 
stereo cameras. The result of which would be effective 
hand tracking models for HCI that are able to run on 
dedicated edge devices that are portable and cheap in 
comparison to high performance desktop computers. 

 
Literature Review 

Pose tracking research covers several focus areas. 
Body pose systems such as BlazePose demonstrate 
near real-time full-skeleton estimation on mobile 
hardware [17], while hand pose  approaches like 
MediaPipe Hands use a two stage palm detector and 
landmark regressor for efficient 2.5D localisation [6]. 
Egocentric vision forms another avenue, with recent 
work addressing self-occlusion and distorted 
perspectives inherent to first person image input[10]. A 
further branch studies tracking how the hand interacts 
with touch objects, where lightweight temporal GCN 
models enable 3D joint tracking under real-time 
constraints [9]. 

These systems make use of a range of input types. RGB 
is the dominant source, enabling low-cost and on device 
mobile processing, as in MediaPipe Hands [6]. Stereo 
RGB extends this by triangulating depth from dual 
cameras, improving geometric understanding as shown 
on embedded devices such as the Jetson Nano [12]. 
RGB-D pipelines further make use of measured depth to 
improve 3D inference under occlusion, with methods like 
HandAugment showing how depth maps enhance 
training and robustness [15]. Each input type introduces 
trade-offs in accuracy, and computational demand, 
which influences application. 

Modern pose estimation models differ in computational 
style. CNNs are observed to be the foundation of many 
lightweight and high performance systems, including 
MediaPipe [6]. Transformer-based architectures exist to 
capture long range dependencies [10]. Hybrid CNN 
transformer models combine local feature extraction with 
global attention, as demonstrated by EdgePose [13]. 
Graph neural networks provide structured reasoning 
over articulated meshes or joint topology, with temporal 
GCNs achieving efficient 3D tracking [9]. 
Encoder-decoder structures also provide a flexible 
template for both lightweight inference and multi-scale 
representation learning [11]. 

Hardware constraints strongly influence the design of 
pose models. High accuracy models often depend on 
RTX-class GPUs for training and evaluation, particularly 
in egocentric and 3D reconstruction tasks [10]. In 
contrast, embedded GPU platforms such as the NVIDIA 
Jetson family support real-time inference in constrained 
environments, including stereo-vision pose systems 
deployed directly on Jetson Nano [12]. Lightweight 
CPU-class devices, such as Raspberry Pi, have 
motivated further reduced-complexity approaches [11], 
while mobile phones remain a major deployment target 
thanks to frameworks like MediaPipe [6]. This range of 
hardware use highlights the foundations for the 
speed-accuracy relationship. 

Across the field, both real and synthetic datasets are 
used. Real datasets such as FPHA and HO-3D capture 
authentic hand object interactions for real world 
generalisation [9]. Synthetic datasets like RHD provide 
dense annotations and controlled variability, enabling 
precise training for pose keypoint identification[14]. 
Depth-based pipelines frequently combine both, using 
synthetic augmentation to improve robustness under 
occlusion or sensor noise [15]. 

Despite these advances, many high-accuracy models 
are impractical for edge devices. Egocentric 3D systems 
often rely on heavy backbones and multi-stage pipelines 
that assume desktop-class compute, resulting in poor 
real-time performance on embedded hardware [10]. 
Lightweight approaches, such as SPLite Hand, 
demonstrate that substantial architecture simplification is 
required for deployment on platforms like the Jetson or 
Raspberry Pi [11]. This divide reflects a persistent 
mismatch between the environments where models are 
trained and those where they must ultimately operate. 

To address edge specific constraints, recent work 
introduces a diverse set of adaptations. Compact CNN 
backbones and low-parameter feature extractors reduce 
FLOPs and memory use [7]. Sparse convolutions further 
minimise computation by operating only on informative 
regions [11]. Decoder stages are simplified into minimal 
heatmap heads or direct regression layers, replacing 
deeper refinement stacks [16]. Lightweight attention 
modules provide global context at low cost [13], while 
cropped ROIs, reduced input resolutions, and detector 
-> tracker pipelines limit unnecessary processing [6]. 
Compression techniques, including pruning and 
quantisation, shrink models for deployment on 
embedded CPUs and mobile GPUs [11], [7]. Collectively, 
these strategies navigate an inherent accuracy-latency 
tradeoff: fine-grained 3D fidelity declines as computation 
is reduced, yet low-latency responsiveness is often more 
critical for interactive applications. 

The literature so far reveals two dominant pathways. 
High precision models suited to desktop GPUs, and 
lightweight pipelines tailored for embedded hardware. 
Lightweight methods, however, are typically restricted to 
single-RGB input using non gpu specific hardware. 
While stereo-based systems, although beneficial for 
depth accuracy, tend to target whole-body tasks rather 
than detailed hand tracking. This leaves a clear gap: the 
absence of a lightweight, real time hand pose tracker 
that leverages both RGB and stereo RGB on modern 
edge devices such as the NVIDIA Jetson paired with a 
ZED 2 stereo camera. Addressing this gap motivates the 
development of stereo enabled, efficiency focused 
hand-pose tracking explicitly designed for constrained 
embedded GPU devices.

Preliminary Software Developed 

A real time hand controlled UI prototype has been 
implemented in hand_11.py. The system uses a ZED 2 
stereo camera for RGB input frames and applies 
MediaPipe Hands to extract 21 landmarks per hand. The 
palm centre is mapped to a screen cursor using 
averages of palm landmarks, while a fist gesture, 
detected from fingertip-to-MCP distances, is treated as a 
click. Interaction is fully touchless, users can navigate 
the interface using only hand motion and simple 
gestures. 

The UI is a full-screen PySide6 application in 
1080×1920 portrait orientation. A dedicated vision 
thread handles camera capture and landmark inference. 
A transparent overlay renders the cursor and hand 
skeleton with temporal smoothing to reduce jitter. The 
hand overlay on the screen is noted to be particularly 
useful to provide the user with visual feedback. An 
inactivity timeout returns the system to the start screen. 

Two demonstration tools have been made. The 
Segmentation Demo displays microscopy imagery and 
highlights regions under the cursor using pre-computed 
connected component masks. The Tone Mapping Demo 
shows “before” and “after” image pairs, revealing the 
enhanced version inside a circular spotlight centred on 
the user’s palm. 

Early Technical Observations 

In decent lighting and at typical desk distances the 
tracking is smooth, provided hand motion is not 
excessively fast. Enabling multiple hand tracking 
reduces performance, so the prototype currently 
assumes a single active hand. Self-occlusion and large 
yaw angles (hand turned sharply sideways) can cause 
brief tracking loss. Tracking quality degrades noticeably 
in low light, and the practical operating range is around 
2–3 m from the camera; beyond this, hand detection 
becomes unusable. Within these limits, the system is 
sufficiently robust to support interaction sessions.
